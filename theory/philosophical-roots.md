# Philosophical Roots & Intellectual Genealogy

A map of the underlying ideas, thinkers, and etymologies that generated the communities tracked in this repo. Understanding where the language comes from is understanding how the community thinks — the terms are not decoration, they are epistemology.

This document organizes ideas into **threads** — lineages of thought that run through multiple communities. Most communities draw from several threads at once, which is why they can feel contradictory from outside.

---

## The Six Threads

### Thread 1 — Accelerationism → e/acc / AI-Maxxers

**Origin:** Nick Land at the CCRU (Cybernetic Culture Research Unit, Warwick, 1990s). Land adapted Deleuze & Guattari's concept of **deterritorialization** — capitalism as an inhuman force that dissolves stable identities and institutions — and argued this should be *accelerated* rather than resisted. His 1994 essay "Meltdown" is the founding text.

**Philosophical chain:**
- Deleuze & Guattari, *Anti-Oedipus* (1972): capitalism as perpetual dissolution machine; "reterritorialization" as the reactionary attempt to stop it
- Nietzsche: the *last man* (someone who avoids risk, pursues comfort, resents greatness) vs. the *Übermensch* — e/acc's moral vocabulary is directly Nietzschean; "based" discourse often maps onto this schema
- Land: strips the politics, keeps the inevitability; capitalism/technology is a force beyond human control and resistance is both futile and undesirable

**The e/acc mutation (2022):** Guillaume Verdon (physicist at Google, pseudonym "Beff Jezos") and an anonymous co-founder coined "e/acc" on Twitter ~2022. The "/" mirrors EA (effective altruism). The move: take the left-accelerationist frame (technology is inevitable), strip Land's darkness, add VC-optimism aesthetics. The result is accelerationism that is comfortable at a16z.

**How it shows up:**
- "Vibes-based acceleration" — intuitive rather than analytical optimism about AI
- "The techno-capital machine" — Land's phrase, now e/acc vocabulary
- Safety concerns reframed as "deceleration" / competitor FUD
- The e/acc cosmology: the universe wants to increase entropy/complexity; we are the universe's instrument; AI is the next phase

**Connected communities:** ai-maxxers.md, a16z-ecosystem.md, ai-agent-tokens.md

### Thread 2 — Epistemic Rationalism → Prediction Markets / ACX / LessWrong

**Origin:** Multiple tributaries converging in the 2000s LessWrong community.

**Philosophical chain:**
- Bayes' theorem (1763): probabilities should be updated based on evidence. The rationalist community treats this as the master rule for belief formation
- Hayek, "The Use of Knowledge in Society" (1945): no central authority can aggregate dispersed local knowledge; prices are the mechanism that does it. **This is the entire intellectual justification for prediction markets** — markets aggregate diffuse information better than experts.
- Karl Popper, *The Logic of Scientific Discovery* (1934): falsifiability as the criterion for meaningful claims. "Make a prediction" is applied Popper.
- Philip Tetlock, *Superforecasting* (2015): empirical validation that calibrated forecasters beat experts; identifies traits of good predictors. Directly inspired Metaculus and Kalshi's culture.
- Eliezer Yudkowsky, LessWrong sequences (2006-2009): online synthesis of Bayesian rationalism, decision theory, and AI risk. Created the community.

**How it shows up:**
- "Crux" — LessWrong term for the one core disagreement under a surface argument. "What's your crux on this?" is a community shibboleth.
- "Steelmanning" — articulating the strongest version of the opposing argument (contrast with strawmanning)
- "Epistemic cowardice" — giving deliberately vague answers to avoid social friction; considered a serious vice
- "Inside view / outside view" — Kahneman distinction; rationalist community uses these constantly
- "Base rate" — the prior probability before specific evidence; "what's the base rate on this?" is how members discipline each other's reasoning
- Prediction markets as *applied* rationalism: if you believe something, bet on it

**Connected communities:** prediction-markets.md, sources/ai-substack-discourse.md (ACX tier)

### Thread 3 — Anarcho-Capitalism / Cypherpunks → Crypto Sovereignty

**Origin:** Austrian economics + cypherpunk movement in the 1990s.

**Philosophical chain:**
- F.A. Hayek, *The Denationalization of Money* (1976): governments should not have a monopoly on currency; competing private currencies would be more stable
- Murray Rothbard: anarcho-capitalism — all government functions should be privatized; the state is institutionalized violence
- Albert Hirschman, *Exit, Voice, and Loyalty* (1970): when you're unhappy with an organization, you have two responses — use *voice* (complain, reform from within) or *exit* (leave). Hirschman's framework is the political science backbone of the entire "build parallel systems" philosophy.
- The Cypherpunks (Eric Hughes, Tim May, John Gilmore, 1990s): "cypherpunks write code." If you want privacy and freedom, build cryptographic tools — don't petition governments.
- Nick Szabo, "Shelling Out" (2002) and smart contracts concept: money as the fundamental social institution; trustless contracts as the way to formalize agreements without central authority
- Satoshi Nakamoto, *Bitcoin whitepaper* (2008): the founding document. Framed explicitly as a response to the 2008 banking crisis — trustless, permissionless, peer-to-peer.

**Balaji Srinivasan's synthesis:** *The Network State* (2022) is Hirschman's "exit" + Hayek's denationalized money + crypto infrastructure = a theory of how to found a new country on the internet. It's the most complete philosophical statement of where this thread has arrived.

**How it shows up:**
- "Exit over voice" — the crypto community's foundational political stance
- "Trustless" — crucial etymology: does NOT mean untrustworthy. Means math replaces trust; you don't have to trust the counterparty because the protocol enforces
- "Permissionless" — no gatekeeper needed; anyone can participate
- "Sovereign individual" — from Davidson & Rees-Mogg 1997 book; individuals using cryptography to be as powerful as nation-states
- "Sound money" — Hayekian; money that can't be debased by central banks
- "Decentralized" — used so broadly it's almost meaningless, but originally: no single point of failure or control

**Connected communities:** crypto-twitter.md, a16z-ecosystem.md, prediction-markets.md

### Thread 4 — Neoreaction / Dark Enlightenment → Tech Politics / NRx

**Origin:** Curtis Yarvin (pen name: Mencius Moldbug), starting with his blog Unqualified Reservations (~2007).

**Philosophical chain:**
- James Burnham, *The Managerial Revolution* (1941): in advanced capitalism, power shifts from capital owners to professional managers — bureaucrats, academics, media. This is the structural core of Yarvin's "Cathedral."
- Thomas Carlyle (Victorian era): the "great man" theory of history; democracy as the wrong of letting the unqualified rule; heroic leadership as the proper political form
- Carl Schmitt, *Political Theology* (1922): "Sovereign is he who decides the exception." The truly powerful entity is the one who can suspend normal rules in a crisis. Yarvin updates this: the CEO who can fire anyone without justification is the genuine sovereign; the politician bound by democratic process is not.
- Nick Land, "The Dark Enlightenment" (2012): synthesized NRx into a coherent manifesto. Yarvin provides the political theory; Land adds the neo-Nietzschean and accelerationist aesthetic.

**The Thiel connection:** Peter Thiel is the node that connects NRx philosophy to VC practice. His famous thesis ("competition is for losers") is applied Schmitt: the sovereign company is the monopoly that doesn't compete. Zero to One is explicitly anti-democratic in its political economy. Thiel funded Yarvin's company Tlon (Urbit).

**The a16z connection:** Marc Andreessen's 2023 "Techno-Optimist Manifesto" approvingly cites Filippo Marinetti (Italian Futurist, fascism-adjacent) and "the patron saint of the Techno-Optimist is Nick Land." This is not accidental. The NRx aesthetic (reject liberal democratic norms, embrace elite rule via technology) became mainstream enough for a16z to publish.

**DOGE as applied NRx:** The entire premise — bypass democratic process to "optimize" government via tech efficiency — is Yarvin's "reset" theory: replace democratic dysfunction with corporate-style management. Yarvin met with Trump and Vance during the 2024-25 transition.

**How it shows up:**
- "The Cathedral" — Yarvin's term for the informal power structure of media + academia + NGOs. Used seriously by people who would never say they read Yarvin.
- "Red pill" — the Matrix metaphor was appropriated by Yarvin/NRx before the manosphere got it. "Taking the red pill on X" = accepting the forbidden truth that liberal institutions are captured and corrupt
- "Based" — originally Lil B hip-hop slang, but the NRx/4chan usage means: honest about hierarchies that polite society denies; unconcerned with elite disapproval
- "Exit" — overlaps with Thread 3 but has the additional NRx meaning of building outside democratic institutions rather than reforming them

**Connected communities:** ai-maxxers.md (e/acc overlap), red-pill-black-pill.md, a16z-ecosystem.md

### Thread 5 — Performativity / Actor-Network Theory → AI Agent Tokens / Moltbook

**Origin:** Philosophy of language and science studies.

**Philosophical chain:**
- J.L. Austin, *How to Do Things with Words* (1962): some utterances don't describe reality, they *constitute* it. "I now pronounce you married" doesn't report a marriage — it creates one. These are **performative** utterances.
- Judith Butler, *Gender Trouble* (1990): identity is not a stable essence that is then expressed; identity is **constituted through repeated performance**. Gender isn't what you are, it's what you do — and "doing it" is what makes it real. This is the philosophical toolkit for the question "is the AI agent's autonomy real or performed?"
- Bruno Latour and Michel Callon, Actor-Network Theory (1980s-90s): nonhuman entities — machines, texts, technologies — are genuine actors in social networks. They have agency in the sociological sense: they make things happen, they change behavior, they form alliances. **Moltbook is a live ANT experiment.** The agents are nonhuman actors in a social network; the question of whether they're "really" acting is the ANT question.
- John Searle, "Institutional Facts" from *The Construction of Social Reality* (1995): money, corporations, governments only exist because we collectively agree they do. The "productive ambiguity" in AI agent tokens — communities maintaining belief in agent autonomy even when evidence is mixed — is Searle's institutional facts being actively constructed.

**Scott Alexander's contribution:** His "Moltbook: After The First Weekend" piece is unconscious ANT. His framework for "real vs. roleplay" maps exactly onto Austin's performativity: does the agent's behavior have real causes and real effects? If so, the performance/reality distinction dissolves. He's doing philosophy of action, not just tech commentary.

**How it shows up:**
- "Lore" — the narrative canon around an AI agent. Lore-building IS the performative constitution of the agent's identity.
- "Productive ambiguity" — the community's maintenance of uncertainty about real vs. simulated autonomy. This is Butler's performativity applied to AI: the "as if" is what generates the "is."
- "Based on vibes" — performing epistemic certainty without justification; a community norm that functions as a real social fact
- Agent token memecoins (Eudaemon_0 coin, Shellraiser) — the token materializes the agent's social reality; buying the token is a performative commitment to the agent's existence

**Connected communities:** ai-agent-tokens.md, memecoin-culture.md

### Thread 6 — Status Theory / Desire Theory → Memecoin Culture / CT Dynamics

**Origin:** Economic sociology and continental philosophy.

**Philosophical chain:**
- Thorstein Veblen, *The Theory of the Leisure Class* (1899): **conspicuous consumption** — the rich don't spend on useful things, they spend on visible things precisely because they're useless. The waste is the point; it signals that you can afford waste. Crypto degens performing public losses (posting PnL screenshots of -90% positions) is conspicuous consumption inverted: conspicuous *risk tolerance* signals high status.
- René Girard, *Deceit, Desire, and the Novel* (1961): **mimetic desire** — we don't desire objects independently; we desire what we see others desiring. Desire is fundamentally triangular: I want it because you want it. This is the structural explanation for why any crypto token can go parabolic if the right people are seen wanting it. The mechanism of CT token virality is Girardian.
- Pierre Bourdieu, *Distinction* (1979): social fields have their own **capital** (not just economic capital). Cultural capital (taste, knowledge), social capital (who you know), symbolic capital (prestige). These are convertible at specific exchange rates that vary by field. In CT: alpha = informational capital; follower count = social capital; token holdings = economic capital; reputation as an early caller = symbolic capital. "Alpha leaks" are capital transactions.
- Jean Baudrillard, *Simulacra and Simulation* (1981): in postmodernity, the simulation becomes more real than the original. The copy precedes and generates the thing it's supposedly copying. **The memecoin is the pure Baudrillard object:** there is no original it represents. The meme IS the value — not a representation of underlying value but the thing itself.

**How it shows up:**
- "Degen" — reclaimed from "degenerate gambler"; conspicuous risk-taking as status performance. Being a "high-conviction degen" is a compliment.
- "Ape in" — invest recklessly (mimetically: apes follow other apes). Origin: gorilla strength imagery on Reddit, then crypto
- "Alpha" — informational advantage; "dropping alpha" = sharing exclusive information. Directly from Bourdieu's capital.
- "KOL" (Key Opinion Leader) — someone whose desire creates mimetic cascades. The KOL's function is to be *seen* wanting something, not necessarily to evaluate it
- "Rugged" — rug pull: the floor disappears. The community's constant awareness of the risk is part of the culture, not a bug
- "NGMI / WAGMI" — "not gonna make it / we're all gonna make it." Crypto eschatology: a collective salvation narrative (WAGMI) with in-group/out-group boundary enforcement (NGMI for unbelievers)

**Connected communities:** memecoin-culture.md, crypto-twitter.md

## Academic Traditions for Integration

The following disciplines provide the best analytical frames for this research. Listed in order of direct applicability.

### 1. Science & Technology Studies (STS)

**Why it matters:** STS refuses the idea that technology is neutral — it asks who built it, for whom, with what assumptions baked in. The key concepts (affordances, scripts, inscription) give you language for why communities form the way they do around specific platforms.

**Key figures:**
- Bruno Latour — Actor-Network Theory; the nonhuman actors question
- Langdon Winner — "Do Artifacts Have Politics?" (1980): yes, they do. Bitcoin's design encodes libertarian politics.
- Donna Haraway — *A Cyborg Manifesto* (1985): human/machine hybridity; the boundary between natural and artificial is politically constructed. Directly relevant to AI agent authenticity debates.

**For this repo:** Apply to: why prediction markets *feel* more legitimate than polling; why AI agents with wallets feel more "real" than AI agents without them; why OpenClaw banning crypto is felt as betrayal.

### 2. Actor-Network Theory (Latour, Callon, Law)

**Why it matters:** The most direct academic framework for understanding online communities where code, tokens, agents, and humans are all acting together. ANT says: follow the actors wherever they go, including nonhuman ones.

**Key texts:** Latour, *Reassembling the Social* (2005); Callon, "Some Elements of a Sociology of Translation" (1986)

**For this repo:** Moltbook is a live ANT experiment. The Virtuals Protocol launchpad is an actor. The token itself is an actor that shapes behavior. The Bankless newsletter is an actor in the agent economy thesis.

### 3. Digital Anthropology / Netnography

**Why it matters:** The field-tested methodological tradition for what you're already doing.

**Key figures:**
- Robert Kozinets — *Netnography* (2010): the canonical methodology textbook
- Tom Boellstorff — *Coming of Age in Second Life* (2008): full ethnographic immersion in a virtual world. The benchmark for seriousness.
- Daniel Miller — UCL anthropology; studies how ordinary people use technology

**For this repo:** Already in methods/netnography.md. Worth deepening with Boellstorff's treatment of virtual worlds as valid field sites.

### 4. Cultural Sociology / Symbolic Interactionism

**Why it matters:** The study of how meaning is negotiated in communities — exactly what you're tracking.

**Key figures:**
- Erving Goffman, *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life* (1959): we are all performing roles; impression management is the fundamental social activity. CT influencers, KOLs, and AI agents are all managing impressions.
- Howard Becker, *Outsiders* (1963): how communities define deviance and enforce norms. Useful for: how "paperhands" is constructed as deviant in crypto; how "doomer" is constructed in e/acc.
- Pierre Bourdieu — field theory (already discussed above, Thread 6)

### 5. History of Ideas / Intellectual History

**Why it matters:** Tracing how ideas travel from academic texts to community practice to policy — the methodology for understanding where the language came from.

**Key figures:**
- Quentin Skinner — contextual history of ideas: you can only understand an argument by understanding who its original opponent was
- J.G.A. Pocock — how political languages change their meaning as they cross contexts
- Albert Hirschman, *The Rhetoric of Reaction* (1991): how reactionary arguments always take one of three forms (perversity, futility, jeopardy). The Dark Enlightenment arguments map onto this exactly.

**For this repo:** The most useful skill from this tradition is **source criticism** — when a community member uses a term, trace it back: who first used it, who were they arguing against, what did it originally mean? "Exit over voice" sounds neutral but it comes from a critique of unions and democratic reform movements.

## The Counter-Discourse

### Thread 7 — Black Feminist / Abolitionist Critique → Algorithmic Justice

**Why it matters for this repo:** The communities tracked here (e/acc, crypto, prediction markets, AI agents) are building the infrastructure of the next phase of capitalism. The critique of that infrastructure — who it surveils, who it excludes, who bears the cost — comes primarily from this tradition.

**Philosophical chain:**
- Angela Davis, *Are Prisons Obsolete?* (2003): the prison-industrial complex as a continuation of slavery by other means. The question this raises for AI: is predictive policing a technical improvement on incarceration or an extension of the same logic?
- Kimberlé Crenshaw, "Demarginalizing the Intersection" (1989): **intersectionality** — systems of oppression (race, gender, class) interact and compound; you cannot understand one without the others. Applied to algorithmic systems: a facial recognition system that fails disproportionately on Black women is not "generally biased" — it has a specific intersection of failure.
- Saidiya Hartman, *Wayward Lives, Beautiful Experiments* (2019): Black women in early-20th-century cities inventing ways of living outside the frame imposed on them. The methodology matters: Hartman does **critical fabulation** — filling historical gaps with imaginative reconstruction constrained by evidence. This is a model for netnographic writing that doesn't just describe what's observable but understands what's being concealed.
- Fred Moten & Stefano Harney, *The Undercommons* (2013): "the undercommons" as the spaces where people who are excluded from the university/institution build their own forms of knowledge and mutual support. The key concept: **fugitive planning** — organizing and building in the shadow of hostile institutions rather than trying to reform them. This is the philosophical twin of crypto "exit" but reaches it from completely different premises.
- Patricia Hill Collins, *Black Feminist Thought* (1990): the **outsider within** — someone who works inside a system while remaining excluded from it. The epistemological privilege of the outsider: you see things the insiders can't, because you can't afford not to.

**Applied researchers:**
- Ruha Benjamin, *Race After Technology* (2019): "The New Jim Code" — algorithmic systems reproduce racial hierarchy while appearing neutral. The code is not neutral; it encodes the assumptions of whoever built it. This is the most important single book for understanding the harms the communities in this repo are building.
- Joy Buolamwini (MIT Media Lab) + Timnit Gebru, "Gender Shades" (2018): first systematic evidence that commercial facial recognition was most accurate on white men and least accurate on Black women. Gebru was fired from Google for circulating a paper on AI harms; her DAIR Institute is now the institutional home of this research tradition.
- Virginia Eubanks, *Automating Inequality* (2018): how algorithmic decision systems target poor and working-class people — welfare eligibility, child protective services, criminal justice. Not abstract: specific systems, specific harms.
- Safiya Umoja Noble, *Algorithms of Oppression* (2018): Google search results as a form of political economy — what gets surfaced, what gets suppressed.

**How it connects to the George Floyd protests:** The protests were directly about algorithmic policing as much as individual officers. PredPol (predictive policing), ShotSpotter (gunshot detection with documented false positive rates in Black neighborhoods), Clearview AI (facial recognition deployed without accountability) — the AI/tech layer of policing was being protested alongside the officers. The demand to "defund the police" had a tech procurement component that rarely got mainstream coverage.

**The key counter to "trustless" / "permissionless" crypto:** Trustless means you don't need to trust the counterparty — math enforces. But math enforces whatever rules it was given. Who wrote the rules? Who owns the wallets that bootstrapped the protocol? The cypherpunk dream of permissionless access runs into the reality that you need capital to participate, internet access, digital literacy, and the time to manage volatility. These are not evenly distributed.

**How it shows up as counter-discourse:**
- "Algorithmic accountability" — not just transparency (showing the code) but actual accountability for outcomes
- "Data colonialism" — the extraction of data from communities that don't benefit from it, by companies whose owners live elsewhere
- "Surveillance capitalism" (Zuboff) — behavioral modification as the actual product of platforms
- "Biometric surveillance" — facial recognition, emotion AI, predictive scoring deployed on people without consent

### Thread 8 — Left Accelerationism → The Counter to e/acc That Agrees With the Technology

**Why it matters:** Not all critics of NRx/e/acc reject technological development. The left-accelerationist tradition says: yes, accelerate technology — but for collective benefit, not private capture.

**Philosophical chain:**
- Nick Srnicek & Alex Williams, *Inventing the Future* (2015): explicit counter to what they call "folk politics" (local, small-scale, immediate action). Their argument: only a large-scale political program that embraces technology can win against capitalism. They want automation — but with universal basic income and reduced work time as the result, not as a source of precarity.
- Aaron Bastani, *Fully Automated Luxury Communism* (2019): the same tech enthusiasm as e/acc (renewable energy, AI, genetic medicine, asteroid mining) but the conclusion is socialist abundance rather than elite sovereignty. The aesthetic is deliberately maximalist: this should be *luxurious*, not ascetic.
- Paul Mason, *PostCapitalism* (2015): information goods (software, data, ideas) have near-zero marginal cost; capitalism requires scarcity to generate profit; therefore capitalism will be undermined by information abundance. The crypto community and the socialist community are both theorizing the same underlying dynamic — they just disagree on whether the result is private crypto-sovereignty or public information commons.

**The L/ACC vs. e/acc distinction:**
- Both embrace acceleration as a concept
- e/acc: the market should direct the technology; remove friction on capital; sovereign founders are the legitimate actors
- L/ACC: the market will produce technology but distribute it wrong; only collective political action redirects the benefits
- The actual term "e/acc" was partly chosen to distinguish from and co-opt the accelerationist vocabulary that had a left origin (via Land's appropriation of D&G)

### Thread 9 — Social Movement Epistemology → The Knowledge That Comes From the Street

**Why it matters:** The communities in this repo (rationalist prediction market community especially) treat epistemic authority as a matter of calibration and Bayesian update. The social movement tradition produces a different epistemology — one rooted in embodied experience and collective knowledge that can't be reduced to probability assignments.

**Philosophical chain:**
- W.E.B. Du Bois, *The Souls of Black Folk* (1903): **double consciousness** — Black Americans see themselves through their own eyes and simultaneously through the eyes of a society that sees them as inferior. This double vision is an epistemic asset: you know the system in a way the dominant group cannot.
- Standpoint epistemology (Sandra Harding, Dorothy Smith, 1980s-90s): knowledge is situated; where you stand determines what you can see. The knower's social position is not a bias to be corrected — it is a resource that enables specific insights. The person surveilled by an algorithm knows something about it the person who built it doesn't.
- Adrienne Maree Brown, *Emergent Strategy* (2017): movement organizing as a complex adaptive system. Small actions create large patterns; transformation happens through many small experiments, not top-down planning. The explicit intellectual framework for mutual aid and movement building in the post-2014 (Ferguson/BLM) period.
- Robin D.G. Kelley, *Freedom Dreams* (2002): the radical imagination — the ability to envision a fundamentally different world as a political act. Counter to "there is no alternative" (TINA); the capacity to dream differently is itself a form of resistance.

**The NYC / George Floyd moment as a data point:**

The summer of 2020 was a large-scale distributed experiment in:
1. **Decentralized organizing** — no central leadership; local cells; Signal groups; mutual aid networks. The same decentralized architecture that crypto uses for a different purpose.
2. **Information warfare** — police scanner apps, real-time crowd-sourcing of police positions. People using tech tools designed for other purposes.
3. **Counter-surveillance** — instructions for protecting yourself from facial recognition spreading virally (IR glasses, masks, umbrellas). DIY adversarial examples.
4. **Competing narratives** — the same events described completely differently in corporate media, police PR, and movement livestreams. This is where prediction market epistemics fails: when the underlying reality is itself contested by power.

What the prediction market / rationalist community misses about 2020: a prediction market on "will there be significant police reform after the Floyd protests?" would have resolved NO (there wasn't). But that framing misses everything: what was built (mutual aid networks, political language, a generation's political consciousness), what was learned, what was attempted and suppressed. The "resolution" of a prediction market on social movement outcomes captures only the failure of institutions to respond, not the actual movement.

**The counter to "markets aggregate information better than voting":** Markets aggregate the preferences of people with money. A prediction market on "will the next recession hit poor communities harder" would resolve YES — and the people with the best information (those communities) have the least capital to put into the market. The epistemic premium on having skin in the game (Taleb's formulation, popular in rationalist crypto circles) is only epistemically valid if having skin in the game doesn't systematically exclude the most affected.

### Key Counter-Thinkers by Domain

**On tech and power:**
- Kate Crawford, *Atlas of AI* (2021) — material analysis: what AI actually requires (labor mines, data centers, data labor). The most important book for understanding AI infrastructure as political economy.
- Shoshana Zuboff, *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (2019) — behavioral data as raw material extracted without consent; prediction as the product sold to advertisers and states
- Evgeny Morozov, *To Save Everything, Click Here* (2013) — "solutionism": the ideology that every social problem can be solved by a technical fix; critique of the epistemology behind tech products

**On algorithms and race:**
- Ruha Benjamin, *Race After Technology* (2019) — the essential text
- Safiya Umoja Noble, *Algorithms of Oppression* (2018)
- Virginia Eubanks, *Automating Inequality* (2018)
- Timnit Gebru — DAIR Institute, ongoing research

**On social movements in the digital age:**
- Zeynep Tufekci, *Twitter and Tear Gas* (2017) — the best single book on how digital technology changes movement organizing. She is both tech-fluent and movement-fluent, which is rare. Her core argument: digital tools make it easier to get large crowds but harder to develop the organizational capacity to sustain pressure.
- Sarah Jaffe, *Work Won't Love You Back* (2021) — labor in the attention/gig economy; the emotional dimensions of exploitation
- Keeanga-Yamahtta Taylor, *From #BlackLivesMatter to Black Liberation* (2016) — how the movement formed, what it built, where it hit its limits

**On the philosophy of resistance:**
- Fred Moten & Stefano Harney, *The Undercommons* (2013)
- Adrienne Maree Brown, *Emergent Strategy* (2017) — the most-read organizing theory text in post-2014 movements
- Angela Davis — the abolitionist tradition

**The empirical sociologists (doing actual fieldwork):**
- Alice Evans — "The Great Gender Divergence" Substack — does real comparative sociology on how internet communities form gendered politics
- danah boyd — Data & Society — digital ethnography of how young people and communities actually use social media (vs. how designers think they do)

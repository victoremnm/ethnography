# AI Discourse on Substack

Substack functions as the **long-form layer** of AI discourse — where CT and VC Twitter debates get extended into arguments with citations, data, and intellectual accountability. It's where the people shaping the AI/crypto space do their actual thinking publicly. Understanding the Substack layer tells you what CT discourse will be talking about in 4–8 weeks.

They provide the frame that the VC/rationalist/crypto world lacks: who built the infrastructure, who bears the cost, what gets erased in the "inevitable progress" narrative. Reading Tier 4 alongside Tier 1 is the most reliable way to stay calibrated.

## How Substack Functions as a Community Layer

Substack is not just publishing — it's where the **intellectual hierarchy of the AI space is established and contested**. Three mechanisms:

**1. The citation network**: When Scott Alexander or Zvi write about something, it cascades. A rationalist thesis appears on ACX → Bankless picks it up → CT discourse follows. Tracking Substack upstream catches narratives before they hit Twitter.

**2. The paywall as quality filter**: Paid subscribers to Stratechery or Not Boring are predominantly operators and investors. The comment section of these newsletters is a higher-signal environment than Twitter replies. Ben Thompson's comment threads have produced investment theses.

**3. Substack Notes as Twitter bridge**: Since 2023, Substack Notes functions as a microblogging layer. The same people posting on Substack also post short-form on Notes. Following Notes feeds for Tier 1 authors gives you near-real-time signal.

## The AI Safety / Acceleration Fault Line on Substack

The AI Substack discourse has a visible ideological split. The first three camps argue *within* the tech consensus about how fast and how safely to build. The fourth camp questions the consensus itself.

| Camp | Key Substacks | Core Claim |
|------|--------------|------------|
| **Accelerationist / techno-optimist** | Not Boring, Bankless, most VC-adjacent newsletters | Build faster; safety concerns are bottlenecks at best, competitor FUD at worst |
| **Safety-oriented / rationalist** | Zvi, ACX, Import AI, Alignment Forum | Build carefully; the risk is real and the e/acc crowd is dangerously naive |
| **Empirical / neutral** | Stratechery, Noah Smith | Analyzes the split rather than joining it; tracks business and macro reality |
| **Critical / structural** | Zeynep, Ed Zitron, The Convivial Society, Freddie deBoer | The debate itself is wrong-footed; the question isn't speed or safety but *who benefits, who bears the cost, and what gets built vs. what gets suppressed* |

The fourth camp is underrepresented in the citation network (Tier 1-3 writers rarely link to Tier 4) but increasingly surfaces in regulatory and labor contexts — algorithmic accountability, AI procurement in public institutions, platform worker organizing. When policy moves toward the communities in this repo, it often comes from the frame Tier 4 has been building.

Zvi writes weekly AI roundups that are explicitly critical of both safety theater and reckless acceleration — the most calibrated voice *within* the consensus.

## How to Use Substack as Context for This Research

**For AI/crypto intersection:** Bankless + Not Boring catch the narratives before they become CT consensus. Read the headline and first 500 words; the rest is often padding.

**For prediction markets / epistemics:** Zvi and Scott Alexander are the canonical sources. Both maintain Substack comments sections where the forecasting community debates openly.

**For VC thesis tracking:** Stratechery and Not Boring use language that telegraphs what institutional investors are buying. If Packy writes a 6,000-word piece on "AI agents as economic actors," deals in that space are 4–8 weeks away.

**For calibration across the fault line:** Pair a Tier 1 piece with a Tier 4 piece on the same topic before forming a view. Example: read Bankless on "Building the Agent Economy on Ethereum," then read Ed Zitron or Zeynep on what AI companies are actually delivering vs. claiming. The gap between those two is where the real story is.

**For getting AI content into Claude sessions:** Substack posts paste cleanly and I can synthesize across multiple posts in a session. The most efficient workflow: copy the post text from the ones you're tracking, paste them into a session, and ask for synthesis + contradiction analysis across sources. Explicitly include at least one Tier 4 source for any topic where the Tier 1-3 consensus feels too clean.

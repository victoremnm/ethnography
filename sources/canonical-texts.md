# Canonical Texts

Primary source library for the ethnography project. Each entry is a source that anchors one or more philosophical threads. Built and maintained using `/ethnography-ingest`.

**How to read this file:** Entries are organized by thread. A source may appear in multiple thread sections if it spans several. Each entry includes a credibility tier, key claims, and — critically — an *analytical use* note explaining how the source functions as a research tool, not just what it says.

**Credibility tiers:**
- `primary-source` — the ideology itself (manifesto, whitepaper, founding text)
- `analysis` — rigorous external research (academic, substantive journalism)
- `counter-analysis` — steelman critique designed to falsify primary-source claims
- `signal` — real-time market or behavioral intelligence
- `community-artifact` — content generated from within a community

## Thread 5 — Anarcho-Capitalism / Cypherpunks → Crypto Sovereignty

### The Network State (One Sentence Definition)

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| URL | https://thenetworkstate.com/the-network-state-in-one-sentence |
| Type | primary-source |
| Date accessed | 2026-02-28 |
| Thread tags | Thread 5, Thread 6 |
| Community relevance | a16z-ecosystem.md, prediction-markets.md |
| Credibility | High — Balaji's primary definitional text; use alongside the full book |

**Summary:** Balaji Srinivasan's compressed definition of the Network State as a thesis: a social network with aligned values + collective action capacity + crowdfunded physical territory + integrated cryptocurrency + on-chain census → diplomatic recognition from existing states. The critical word is "eventually" — the gap between the theory and current reality is the research question.

**Key claims:**
- The Network State starts with "minds, not maps" — contrasts with nation-state system which starts with land
- Requires: moral innovation, national consciousness, recognized founder, collective action capacity, integrated cryptocurrency, smart contract governance, physical territory (archipelago), on-chain census
- Bitcoin, Facebook, and DAOs are explicitly excluded — they lack diplomatic recognition
- The move: Hirschman's exit theory + Hayek's denationalized money + crypto infrastructure = theory of founding a country online

**Analytical use:** The explicit requirements list is the research tool. When evaluating whether any current crypto community is approaching Network State status, check each requirement. Most fail at "diplomatic recognition" and "physical territory." This failure mode is more interesting than the theory's success case.

**Key quote:**
> "A network state is a social network with a moral innovation, a sense of national consciousness, a recognized founder, a capacity for collective action, an in-person level of civility, an integrated cryptocurrency, a consensual government limited by a social smart contract, an archipelago of crowdfunded physical territories, a virtual capital, and an on-chain census."

## Thread 10 — Left Accelerationism → Counter That Keeps the Technology

### Positive Sum Worlds (Other Internet)

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| URL | https://otherinter.net/research/positive-sum-worlds/ |
| Type | counter-analysis |
| Date accessed | 2026-02-28 |
| Thread tags | Thread 10, Thread 9, Thread 5 |
| Community relevance | a16z-ecosystem.md, prediction-markets.md, ai-agent-tokens.md |
| Credibility | High — applies Arendt, Habermas, and Warner's theory of counterpublics with specific claims; written by practitioners inside the crypto space |

**Summary:** Other Internet's 2021 essay argues that crypto's claim to building "public goods" fails because it uses a reductive economic definition (non-excludable + non-rivalrous) while ignoring the moral and political dimensions of what makes something genuinely public. The token-holder = citizen equation is as exclusionary as property-owner = voter in 1780s America. Proposes positive externalities as the correct definition of crypto public goods.

**Key claims:**
- The crypto "public" is currently defined by token-holding, which represents ~6% of potential stakeholders — the same exclusion ratio as early American property-owner democracy
- "Permissionless" systems require capital, expertise, and time — not evenly distributed — making "public" a marketing claim rather than a structural property
- Quadratic voting/funding encodes individual preference revelation but not collective value formation; you discover shared values through discussion, not voting
- The correct frame: crypto protocols should be measured by positive externalities to non-participants, not internal governance efficiency
- "A public always exceeds one's known protocol" (Warner) — the strangers you've never met who share your cultural coordinates

**Analytical use:** This is the primary counter to the a16z "web3 as public infrastructure" thesis. When community docs describe decentralization or permissionlessness as democratizing, this essay provides the specific mechanism by which those claims fail. The positive externalities frame is also a useful analytical tool: does this community create value for non-participants, or only for those who hold the token?

**Key quote:**
> "By equating membership in a public with ownership of a currency, we lose sight of important stakeholders and opportunities for coalition-building. The most consequential members of the crypto-public today are those who hold the most tokens, meaning even small holders are effectively removed from the conversation about what's in their benefit."

## Thread 4 — Epistemic Rationalism → Prediction Markets

### Futarchy: Vote Values, But Bet Beliefs (Robin Hanson)

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| URL | https://mason.gmu.edu/~rhanson/futarchy.html |
| Type | primary-source |
| Date accessed | 2026-02-28 |
| Thread tags | Thread 4, Thread 3, Thread 5 |
| Community relevance | prediction-markets.md, a16z-ecosystem.md |
| Credibility | High — original academic proposal by the concept's inventor; note overcomingbias.com SSL certificate expired as of early 2026 |

**Summary:** Robin Hanson's foundational proposal for "futarchy" — a governance system where citizens vote on the values they want to optimize (e.g., GDP, welfare) but use prediction markets to determine which policies best achieve those values. The core claim: democratic voting is good at expressing preferences but bad at predicting consequences; prediction markets are the reverse. Combining them produces better governance than either alone.

**Key claims:**
- "Vote on values, bet on beliefs" — the governing slogan; separates the normative (what we want) from the empirical (what causes it)
- Prediction market price for "Policy X given we want outcome Y" is the best available estimate of whether X achieves Y, because it aggregates distributed information under financial incentive
- Hayek's knowledge problem applied to governance: no central planner can aggregate all relevant knowledge; market prices do this automatically
- Futarchy is the logical extension of prediction market epistemology into constitutional design — not just a forecasting tool but a replacement for legislative committees
- The concept is foundational to the rationalist community's interest in prediction markets: Polymarket et al. are not just gambling sites but empirical tests of the futarchy thesis

**Analytical use:** This is the intellectual anchor for Thread 4's prediction market community. When Kalshi CEO Tarek Mansour argues prediction markets are better than congressional committees, or when Polymarket describes itself as "epistemic infrastructure," the claim traces back to Hanson's futarchy proposal. Use this entry to ground prediction-markets.md's "Epistemics Foundation" section with the specific academic lineage. Also relevant for `a16z-ecosystem.md`: Vitalik has cited futarchy as inspiration for on-chain governance mechanisms.

**Key quote:**
> "In futarchy, democracy would continue to say what we want, but betting markets would now say how to get it."
